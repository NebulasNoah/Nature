Whats the difference between a Honey Bee and any other kind of Bee?

There are roughly 274 species of wild bee, With 24 of them being extant bumble bees and 250 of them being solidary bees. Solidary bees are bees that live on there own, they do not form groups/hives. extant bumble bees are of the genius bombus and they do live form hives.

Compared to the large variaty of bumble bee species there is only one species of honey bee, Apis Mellifera, There are different sub species and some colour variation which will often have different common names. Although while they may have a different common name they are all still the same species. Dispite being only one species, the honey bee is not endangered and its population has only grown in recent years.

The key difference between bumble bees and honey bees is that bumble bees do not produce honey. The reason that they do not is because during the summer there queens will mate and then during the winter the queen hibernates while the rest of the hive dies, she will then wake up in the spring for this reason there is no point for them to make honey. Honey bees on the other hand do not do this and need to create enough food to survive over the winter and they do this by producing honey.

What is so bad about honey bees?

As mentioned before, Honey bees reduce the biodiversity of where ever they are farmed. There are many reasons for this, Here are just a few of the main reasons:

Plant Optimisation : What this means is that farmers have found out that certian flowers will produce more pollen that the bees can use to produce more honey. Because of this it is common for farmers to only grow one species of flower that is most optimised for there bees this leads to a drop in the plant diversity around where bees are commonly farmed.

Competition : Honey bee colonies have been found to collect between 10.5 and 46.75 kg of pollen annually, This means that any area with a honey bee colony will see a dramatic decrease in the amount of pollen that is avalble to other species of bee and insect that use the pollen as a food source. Many bumble bee species hae even been shown to change their behaviour and go for lower pollen count plants to avoid direct competition with honey bees. It has been shown that in areas where honey Bee populations are controlled or lowered, wild bee populations are higher and more diverse.

Disease Transmission : Several diseases affecting honeybees can be found in bumblebees. The seriousness to bumblebees of most of these diseases is not yet precisely known, but bumblebees infected with Deformed Wing Virus (DWV) are known to develop deformed wings and suffer higher mortality. Bumblebees have also been found carrying the fungal pathogen Nosema ceranae, a new disease of honeybees, and infectious diseases have been implicated in bumblebee declines.
Disease transfer takes place mostly through the shedding of disease particles onto shared flowers. DWV is the best-studied of these diseases, and the human-caused movement of managed honeybees has been found to be the source of DWV outbreaks in bumblebees. As many other diseases are likely to be spread in a similar fashion, it is reasonable to assume that having managed bees in close proximity to vulnerable populations of wild bees will present a high risk of infecting these wild bees with new diseases.

What can be done to reduce the negative impacts of honey bees?

Plant Diversity : One way to reduce the impact of honey bees on bio diversity is to inssure that even in areas where honey bees are farmed, farmers should plant a wide variaty of Plant species. This will help ensure a more diverse eco system which encourages the growth of various pollinator species.

Hive Positioning : It is vital that bee keepers avoid putting hives in enviroments where endangered species of wild bee live as the competition between the honey and wild bees will likely cause the endangered species to either go extinct or sevearly reduce there numbers. As such it is important to servay the area and be awear of what polinators exist in an area before placing a bee hive and inssure that they are spaced in a way that it still allows room for wild bee populations.

Buy Less or no honey : The reason farmers are able to have more honey bees is because there is growing demand for more honey, if consumers ate less honey then it would mean farmers have less insentive to build more hives as profit margines would be lower.

Look out for pettitions to sign : There are various orginisations that will often put out pettitions for people to sign for things such as reducing the amount of harmful pestisides used to protecting an area with endangered bee species within them. How is honey harvested?

There are many ways to harvest the honey from the hives, some more ethical than others. I will detail one of the ethical ways that honey can be harvested, this method is usually used by small scale bee keepers and hobbiests.

During what most would consider a more ethical way of farming in order To harvest honey, beekeepers pull a frame from the hive and use a hot knife to cut away the wax. Many keep the wax to make candles.

The next step in the process involves a honey extractor. This is a device that takes the liquid honey out of a honeycomb without destroying the frame. That way, the frame can be put back into the hive and used again. Once the honey sits for a few days, it’s ready to bottle and eat.

When harvesting honey, it’s important to only take the extra honeycombs the bees don’t need. That’s especially true when beekeepers harvest during fall. Bees need honey to survive over the winter. If you take too much, the hive may not make it.

However, responsible harvesting doesn’t harm bees. Beekeepers work hard to protect their hives and do their best not to remove too much honey. If you’re thinking of keeping bees, spend plenty of time learning about how to care for a hive. It’s best to get advice from an expert before you start.

After all, bees play an important part in our ecosystem. It’s important for people to do what they can to help them. That’s especially true for beekeepers who take responsibility for their care. What is honey?

Nectar is the main ingredient for honey and also the main source of energy for bees. Using a long straw-like tongue called a proboscis, honey bees suck up nectar droplets from the flower’s special nectar-making organ, called the nectary.

When the nectar reaches the bee’s honey stomach, the stomach begins to break down the complex sugars of the nectar into more simple sugars that are less prone to crystallization, or becoming solid. This process is called “inversion”.

Once a worker honey bee returns to the colony, it passes the nectar onto another younger bee called a house bee (between 12-17 days old).

House bees take the nectar inside the colony and pack it away in hexagon-shaped beeswax honey cells. They then turn the nectar into honey by drying it out using a warm breeze made with their wings.

Once the honey has dried out, they put a lid over the honey cell using fresh beeswax – kind of like a little honey jar. In the winter, when the flowers have finished blooming and there’s not as much nectar available, the bees can open this lid and share the honey they saved.
